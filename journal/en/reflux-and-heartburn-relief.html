Reflux and Heartburn Relief

By Michael Guralnik · June 2026 · 5 min read

That warm, rising burn behind the breastbone after a good meal. The sour taste at the back of the throat when you lie down. A cough that won't quite explain itself. Reflux is one of the most common complaints people quietly live with — often for years — assuming it's simply part of how their body works. Sometimes it is something to accept, but very often there's room to feel considerably better.

What's actually happening

Between your oesophagus (the tube that carries food down) and your stomach sits a ring of muscle called the lower oesophageal sphincter. Its job is simple: open to let food in, then close to keep stomach contents where they belong. Reflux happens when that ring relaxes at the wrong moment, allowing acidic stomach contents to travel back upward. The stomach lining is built to handle acid; the oesophagus is not. That mismatch is what you feel as heartburn.

Occasional reflux is normal and harmless. When it becomes frequent or persistent, it's usually called GERD (gastro-oesophageal reflux disease) — and that's worth paying attention to.

Why it happens

The causes are rarely single. Common contributors include eating large or rushed meals, lying down too soon after eating, carrying extra weight around the middle, certain foods (fatty or fried meals, coffee, alcohol, chocolate, mint, very spicy dishes), smoking, some medications, and a hiatal hernia, where part of the stomach pushes up through the diaphragm. And — more on this shortly — stress plays a larger role than most people realise.

Two faces of the same fire

Here's a distinction that surprises many people. Heartburn feels like too much acid, and for many that's exactly what it is. But not always.

In the high-acidity picture, the stomach produces plenty of acid, and the problem is mechanical: the sphincter isn't holding, so acid escapes upward. This is the version most antacids and acid-blocking medications are designed for, and for many people they bring real relief.

In the low-acidity picture, the issue is too little stomach acid. The thinking goes that without enough acid, food sits and ferments rather than moving on, building pressure that pushes contents upward, while the sphincter doesn't get the strong acidic signal it needs to close firmly. People in this group sometimes notice bloating, fullness, and a sense that meals "just sit there."

Why does the distinction matter? Because the two can call for opposite approaches, and what soothes one may aggravate the other. The honest answer is that telling them apart isn't always straightforward, and self-diagnosis has limits. If reflux is a regular part of your life, this is genuinely worth exploring with a doctor or a qualified practitioner rather than guessing.

The stress connection — and your vagus nerve

If you've ever noticed your stomach tightening during a stressful week, you've already felt the link. Digestion runs largely on the parasympathetic nervous system — your "rest and digest" mode. Stress flips you into "fight or flight," and digestion is one of the first things the body deprioritises when it thinks you're in danger.

At the centre of this sits the vagus nerve, the long wandering nerve that connects brain and gut. It helps set the tone of the oesophageal sphincter, governs the rhythm of stomach movement, and influences acid secretion. When vagal activity is healthy and steady, the digestive system tends to work smoothly. When you're chronically stressed, that signalling becomes erratic — motility slows, the sphincter behaves less reliably, and reflux finds an opening.

This is encouraging news, in a way: it means reflux isn't only about what you eat, but about the state you're in when you eat it. And the state of your nervous system is something you can influence.

Eating habits that help

Small changes to how you eat often matter as much as what's on the plate:

Eat smaller meals. A full stomach puts pressure on the sphincter. Smaller, more frequent meals ask less of it.

Don't eat right before bed. Lying down removes gravity's help. Aim to finish eating around three hours before lying down, so your stomach has time to empty.

Slow down. Eating quickly means swallowing air and under-chewing, which adds pressure and makes digestion harder. A few unhurried minutes before the first bite — even three slow breaths — shifts you toward that "rest and digest" state.

Create joy around your dining table. A meal that is tasty, beautifully served and eaten with no rush reduces the stress, slows down the process, and enables a mindful environment around food and eating. What could be wrong with that? 
Avoid processed food. Food coming of a shelf in a package often contains ingredients you never expected to be there, and many of the ingredients added to it may not agree with your system. In some industrialized products, these added substances can be downright unhealthy.

Notice your triggers. Coffee, alcohol, large fatty meals, and very spicy or acidic foods are common culprits, but everyone's list is personal. A short food diary often reveals patterns faster than guesswork.

Raise the head of the bed slightly if night-time reflux is an issue — a wedge under the mattress works better than stacking pillows.

A word on supplements

These can support comfort, but they're not a substitute for sorting out the underlying picture — and a few interact with medications or conditions, so it's worth a conversation with a healthcare provider first, especially if you're already on acid-reducing medication.

iDGL (deglycyrrhizinated licorice) s a traditional soothing remedy for the digestive lining, with the blood-pressure-raising compound removed.

Slippery elm and marshmallow root form a gentle, protective coating that many find calming for an irritated throat and oesophagus.

Melatonin is best known for sleep, but there's interesting evidence it also helps protect the oesophageal lining.

Magnesium supports muscle function and relaxation, and is gentle for most people.

Breathing your way to a calmer gut

This is where something genuinely simple meets something genuinely effective. The diaphragm — your main breathing muscle — also wraps around the oesophagus and acts as a natural support to that sphincter. Slow, deep belly breathing does two things at once: it strengthens that diaphragmatic support, and it stimulates the vagus nerve, nudging you into digestive mode.

A practice to try, ideally before meals and again before bed:

Sit or lie comfortably with one hand on your belly.

Breathe in slowly through the nose for about four counts, letting the belly rise — not the chest.

Exhale gently for about six counts, longer than the in-breath. The extended exhale is what most strongly engages the vagus nerve.

Continue for five to ten minutes.

Done regularly, diaphragmatic breathing is one of the few reflux strategies with both research support and no downside. It's also the thread connecting much of what we explore in our movement and Qigong work, where breath and a settled nervous system are the starting point for the body working well.

When to see a doctor

Reflux is usually a comfort issue, but some signs deserve prompt medical attention: difficulty or pain when swallowing, unexplained weight loss, vomiting, black or bloody stools, or symptoms that persist despite changes and over-the-counter remedies. And because chest discomfort can occasionally signal something to do with the heart rather than the stomach, anything intense, unfamiliar, or accompanied by breathlessness or arm or jaw pain should be checked without delay.

The bigger picture

Reflux sits right at the meeting point of body and mind — diet, posture, and the state of your nervous system all converge in that one burning sensation. Small, consistent adjustments to how you eat, how you breathe, and how you carry stress can shift things meaningfully. Calming the system that governs digestion is exactly the kind of whole-person work — through breath, movement, and easing the grip of stress on the body — that sits at the heart of what we do.
